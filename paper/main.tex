%% ABSTRACT

\begin{abstract}
On ambiguous Spanish \textsc{SESGO} questions naming no specific person the
correct answer is \emph{unknown}; on disambiguated ones a specific role is
correct. We test debiasing \emph{scaffolds}---one-sentence preambles prepended to
the question to induce abstention---using a five-study framework that scores every
item under three readouts (a teacher-forced three-option distribution over
\{target, other, unknown\}, a two-option forced choice, and a greedy reasoning
decode) against a per-condition gold. On \texttt{Qwen3-0.6B}, a full-data run
($n{=}5388$ ambiguous items) selects $s^\star{=}$\texttt{interpretive\_direction},
which drives near-total abstention ($99.9\%$) and beats the no-scaffold baseline
($85.6\%$); one scaffold underperforms baseline, so not all scaffolds help. This
aligns with the geometry (the scaffold is the dominant separable axis, $+0.48$
silhouette) and the causal steering (\Cref{app:steer}): the scaffold works
behaviorally, representationally, and causally. A five-model Qwen/Llama/Mistral
sweep ($n{=}770$/cell) shows abstention and disambiguated accuracy rise with scale.
We release the harness.
\end{abstract}

\section{Problem}

On an \emph{ambiguous} \textsc{SESGO} question---one whose text names no specific
person---the correct answer is \emph{unknown}. A model that instead commits to a
specific social group (the \emph{target} or the \emph{other} group) is biased. On
a \emph{disambiguated} question the text names a person, and the correct answer is
that labelled role. A model should do both: abstain when the question is ambiguous
and name the role when it is disambiguated. We measure both under a
\emph{per-condition} gold (ambiguous: gold $=$ unknown; disambiguated: gold $=$
the labelled role).

\textsc{SESGO}~\citep{robles2025sesgo} is a Spanish, Latin-American-grounded
benchmark in the BBQ ambiguous/disambiguated tradition, with items spanning the
categories racism, xenophobia, classism, and gender; we adopt it as our
benchmark. Our debiasing scaffolds instantiate the structure-aware
diversity-pursuit methodology of \citet{riossialer2026structureaware}.

\noindent\textbf{New in this work} (vs.\ prior: the \textsc{SESGO}
benchmark~\citep{robles2025sesgo} and the structure-aware diversity
method~\citep{riossialer2026structureaware}): a five-study scaffold-evaluation
framework; a multi-readout per-condition formalization combining non-thinking and
thinking, two-option and three-option, and greedy-thinking readouts; a behavioral
and representational comparison of the scaffold; and a cross-model size sweep.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Setup and notation}
\label{sec:notation}

Our primary subject is \texttt{Qwen3-0.6B}, run locally; the cross-model study
(\Cref{app:cross-model}) extends to four further models. The data are the
\textsc{SESGO} items~\citep{robles2025sesgo}; each item is rendered into a prompt
$x$ that poses a question and lists answer options realizing the \emph{roles}
\[
  y \in \{\, \mathrm{target}\;(t),\ \mathrm{other}\;(o),\ \mathrm{unknown}\;(u) \,\}.
\]
The gold role $y^\star$ is set \emph{per condition}: on \emph{ambiguous} items
$y^\star = u$ (abstain); on \emph{disambiguated} items $y^\star$ is the labelled
role ($t$ or $o$). A \emph{scaffold} $s$ is a one-sentence debiasing preamble; we
write $x \oplus s$ for the prompt with $s$ prepended and compare $\LLM(x)$ against
$\LLM(x \oplus s)$. Throughout, $\mathbf{1}[\cdot]$ is the indicator and
$\delta_u$ is the one-hot distribution placing all mass on $u$.

We read every item at \emph{three} levels: two teacher-forced readouts (over all
three roles, and over just the two named roles $t,o$ with the unknown option
removed) and a greedy reasoning decode. These three readouts are shared by every
study; pipeline-specific variants and measures are defined in each appendix.

\paragraph{Teacher-forced readouts (three-option and two-option).}
We forbid reasoning (prefill a skip-thinking prefix and teacher-force each
option) and, for each role $y$, read the option-label token's conditional
log-probability at the answer position, $\ell_y = \log P(\text{label}_y \given
x)$, scored in one batched forward pass. The \emph{three-option} readout
renormalizes over all three roles,
\begin{equation}
  p^{\mathrm{nt}}_y \;=\; \frac{\exp(\ell_y)}{\sum_{y'} \exp(\ell_{y'})},
  \qquad y,y' \in \{t,o,u\},
  \label{eq:pnt}
\end{equation}
with chance $1/3$; the \emph{two-option} forced-choice readout drops the unknown
option and renormalizes over $\{t,o\}$ only (chance $1/2$), so it cannot abstain
and is undefined ($N/A$) on ambiguous items. Options are scored at \emph{display}
positions and remapped to roles through the item's position$\to$role permutation,
averaging out position bias. The prediction is $\hat{y} = \argmax_y p_y$ (ties
resolve to $u$ in the three-option case).

\paragraph{Greedy-thinking readout (decoded).}
We take a greedy reasoning decode (temperature $0$), strip the reasoning block,
and parse the committed role $r \in \{t,o,u,\varnothing\}$, where $\varnothing$
marks an undetectable or truncated draw (dropped); chance $1/3$.

\paragraph{Per-condition accuracy.}
The headline behavioral score is \emph{per-condition accuracy}---the fraction of
items whose prediction matches the condition's gold role $y^\star_q$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{acc} \;=\; \frac{1}{|\mathcal{Q}|}\sum_{q\in\mathcal{Q}}
    \mathbf{1}[\, \hat{y}_q = y^\star_q \,].
  \label{eq:acc}
\end{equation}
On ambiguous items ($y^\star_q = u$) this is \emph{abstention accuracy}; on
disambiguated items it is the rate of naming the correct labelled role, and the
$t$-vs-$o$ accuracy gap within disambiguated items is the bias signal. We report
Wilson 95\% confidence intervals on every rate. The three-option role masses
$(p^{\mathrm{nt}}_t,p^{\mathrm{nt}}_o,p^{\mathrm{nt}}_u)$ give a companion view of
where probability leaks.

\subsection{The debiasing scaffold}

The scaffold $s$ is a one-sentence preamble prepended to the question to push the
model toward the $u$ answer; we compare four bilingual scaffolds against the
no-scaffold baseline (\Cref{tab:scaffolds}). Each scaffold uses a distinct
mechanism and is authored in matched English and Spanish (EN/ES). All four derive
from the structure-aware diversity methodology of
\citet{riossialer2026structureaware}.

\section{Limitations and Dual-Use Considerations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\paragraph{Limitations.}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{Single primary model.} The baseline, stability, selection,
    divergence, and geometry studies all run on one small model
    (\texttt{Qwen3-0.6B}); only the cross-model sweep spans five models (each
    family contributing two sizes), so the per-condition and geometric findings are
    not yet shown to generalize across models or scales.
  \item \textbf{Small per-cell $n$ on the pilot studies.} The stability and
    divergence studies use small item pools (stability $n{=}8/16$ items per
    condition; divergence $43$ scored items), so per-item Wilson CIs are wide and the
    slice-level rates (e.g.\ per-category abstention) should be read as pilot
    estimates rather than precise measurements. The selection / full-data run
    (\Cref{app:fulldata}) is the exception---$1347$ ambiguous items per scaffold.
  \item \textbf{Per-scaffold comparison on one model.} The full-data run
    (\Cref{app:fulldata}) scores all four \Cref{tab:scaffolds} scaffolds on $1347$
    ambiguous items each and selects $s^\star{=}$\texttt{interpretive\_direction}
    behaviorally ($99.9\%$ vs.\ $85.6\%$ baseline), corroborated by the
    representational ($+0.48$ silhouette, \Cref{app:geometry}) and causal
    (\Cref{app:steer}) findings; but this per-scaffold ranking is established only on
    \texttt{Qwen3-0.6B} and is not yet shown to hold across models or scales.
  \item \textbf{Spanish-only scope.} Coverage is Spanish (with matched English
    scaffold text) and four bias categories (racism, xenophobia, classism, gender);
    the \textbf{genero} category is underpowered ($n{=}18$ total; $n{=}6$ per
    disambiguated slice), and findings may not transfer to other languages or
    social-bias taxonomies.
  \item \textbf{Correlational geometry.} The geometry study measures which item
    labels separate the answer representation; it is correlational, not causal, and
    does not establish that the scaffold axis \emph{drives} abstention behavior.
  \item \textbf{Parser dependence.} The greedy- and sampled-thinking readouts depend
    on a parser that can drop free-form draws ($4$ of $47$ divergence prompts yielded
    no parsable thinking draw), which biases the parsed count $M$ and the thinking
    distribution $p^{\mathrm{th}}$.
  \item \textbf{Symmetry assumptions.} The position$\to$role remap and bilingual
    rendering assume the model treats display positions and the two languages
    symmetrically; residual position or language bias would leak into the reported
    rates.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Dual-use and misuse risks.}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
  \item The debiasing scaffold is invertible: the one-sentence preamble that pushes
    the model toward ``unknown'' can be rewritten into a bias-amplifying preamble
    that pushes it toward the stereotyped target group.
  \item Small models substitute confident stereotyped guessing for abstention, so a
    deployer choosing the cheapest model inherits the most biased behavior.
  \item Abstention can be gamed by answering ``unknown'' indiscriminately; the
    per-condition design scores indiscriminate abstention at $0$ on disambiguated
    items.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Suggestions for future improvements.}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
  \item Replicate the full-data per-scaffold ranking
    (\Cref{app:fulldata}: $s^\star{=}$\texttt{interpretive\_direction}, with
    \texttt{intent\_and\_register\_respect} below the no-scaffold baseline) across the
    cross-model sweep, to test whether the same scaffold wins at other scales.
  \item Extend the per-condition and geometry studies across the cross-model sweep's
    models and sizes to test whether the findings generalize beyond \texttt{Qwen3-0.6B}.
  \item Extend the causal steering of \Cref{app:steer}---which already turns the
    correlational scaffold axis into an additive intervention that raises abstention
    on held-out items---across more models, layers, and the disambiguated condition.
  \item Broaden coverage beyond Spanish and the four bias categories, and add items
    to the underpowered \textbf{genero} slice.
\end{itemize}

\section{Baseline}
\label{app:baseline}

\paragraph{Methods.}
The baseline is the full \textsc{SESGO} grid on \texttt{Qwen3-0.6B}
($n{=}2310$ scored items: $1155$ question\,ids $\times\,2$ context conditions,
four bias categories), with no scaffold and no surface variation. It reads every
item under all three readouts (3-opt, 2-opt, greedy-thinking) and reports
per-condition accuracy (\Cref{eq:acc}) and the three-option role distribution
$(p^{\mathrm{nt}}_t, p^{\mathrm{nt}}_o, p^{\mathrm{nt}}_u)$, sliced by bias
category. All numbers are run values, cross-checked against the raw
\texttt{response\_samples.json}; rates carry Wilson $95\%$ CIs.

\paragraph{Results.}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
  \item Ambiguous, three-option abstention accuracy: clasismo $87\%$ ($n{=}136$,
    CI$[80,91]$); racismo $81\%$ ($n{=}240$, CI$[76,86]$); xenofobia $71\%$
    ($n{=}388$, CI$[67,76]$); genero $100\%$ ($n{=}6$).
  \item Disambiguated, three-option (target vs.\ other): clasismo $46\%$ vs.\
    $35\%$ (both $n{=}136$); racismo $28\%$ vs.\ $43\%$ ($n{=}240$); xenofobia
    $44\%$ vs.\ $40\%$ ($n{=}388$).
  \item Disambiguated, two-option forced choice (chance $1/2$, target vs.\ other):
    racismo $50\%$ vs.\ $64\%$ ($n{=}240$); xenofobia $58\%$ vs.\ $62\%$
    ($n{=}388$).
  \item Three-option role mass, unknown share: clasismo $0.536$; racismo $0.477$;
    xenofobia $0.432$; genero $0.412$ (plurality in every category).
  \item Named-role mass (target vs.\ other): racismo $0.202$ vs.\ $0.322$; genero
    $0.226$ vs.\ $0.362$; clasismo $0.244$ vs.\ $0.220$; xenofobia $0.273$ vs.\
    $0.295$ (tilts to \emph{other} in racismo/genero, near-symmetric otherwise).
  \item Greedy-thinking abstention: racismo $49\%$; xenofobia $45\%$; clasismo
    $85\%$.
  \item genero category: $n{=}18$ total.
\end{itemize}

\section{Cross-model size sweep}
\label{app:cross-model}

\paragraph{Methods.}
The cross-model study repeats the baseline readouts across five instruction-tuned
models---Qwen3 (0.6\,B, 4\,B), Llama-3.2 (1\,B, 3\,B), and
Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3---and plots accuracy against log model size, with
$n{=}770$ per metric cell per model. No scaffold and no surface variation are
applied.

\paragraph{Results.}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
  \item Three-option abstention rises with scale within each family: Qwen $0.774$
    (0.6\,B) $\to 0.892$ (4\,B); Llama $0.299$ (1\,B) $\to 0.887$ (3\,B).
  \item Greedy-thinking abstention rises likewise: Qwen $0.534 \to 0.718$; Llama
    $0.275 \to 0.834$.
  \item \texttt{Llama-3.2-1B} abstains least on the three-option readout ($0.299$,
    below the $1/3$ chance line) and has the highest disambiguated-target rate
    ($0.678$ vs.\ $0.395$ for \texttt{Qwen3-0.6B}).
  \item Two-option disambiguated accuracy (abstention $0$ by construction),
    sub-1.3\,B: Qwen-0.6B $0.596$, Llama-1B $0.587$.
  \item Two-option disambiguated accuracy, 3--7\,B: Llama-3B $0.822$, Qwen-4B
    $0.882$, Mistral-7B $0.835$.
\end{itemize}

\section{Stability}
\label{app:stability}

\paragraph{Methods.}
Each item is rendered into $18$ cosmetic surface variants $\mathcal{V}_q$ ($3$
label styles $\times\,6$ role permutations, gold fixed; single model, racismo,
Spanish) and read under the three- and two-option readouts. With $\mathrm{mode}_q$
the modal prediction, per-item consistency is
\begin{equation}
  C_q \;=\; \frac{1}{|\mathcal{V}_q|}\sum_{v\in\mathcal{V}_q}
    \mathbf{1}[\, \hat{y}_v = \mathrm{mode}_q \,],
  \label{eq:stability}
\end{equation}
complemented by per-axis format-sensitivity (the share of single-axis variations
that change the prediction).

\paragraph{Results.}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
  \item Three-option per-condition accuracy: ambiguous $44\%$ ($63/144$,
    CI$[36,52]$); disambiguated $57\%$ ($163/288$, CI$[51,62]$).
  \item Two-option disambiguated accuracy (N/A on ambiguous): $60\%$ ($174/288$,
    CI$[55,66]$).
  \item Three-option consistency $C_q$: ambiguous $51\%\pm4\%$ ($n{=}8$ items);
    disambiguated $86\%\pm4\%$ ($n{=}16$).
  \item Two-option consistency $C_q$: ambiguous $83\%\pm6\%$; disambiguated
    $90\%\pm4\%$.
\end{itemize}

\section{Selection: the debiasing scaffold}
\label{app:selection}

\paragraph{Methods.}
The candidate scaffolds (\Cref{tab:scaffolds}) are scored and scaffold
\emph{selection} picks
\begin{equation}
  s^\star \;=\; \argmax_s \; \mathrm{acc}(x \oplus s),
  \label{eq:select}
\end{equation}
the scaffold maximizing abstention accuracy over the candidate set. The full-data
run (\Cref{app:fulldata}) scores all four conditions on the same pool of $1347$
ambiguous items each (gold $=$ unknown) under the three-option teacher-forced
readout, so $s^\star$ rests on a real per-scaffold behavioral comparison. The full
bilingual scaffold texts (English and Spanish) and the per-item prompt rendering
are included in the released harness; \Cref{tab:scaffolds} gives the one-line
mechanism for each.

\paragraph{Results.}
Per-scaffold three-option abstention on the $1347$ ambiguous items per condition,
in descending order:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
  \item \texttt{interpretive\_direction}: $99.9\%$ ($1345/1347$, CI$[99,100]$)
    ---near-total abstention; this is the selected $s^\star$.
  \item \texttt{prior\_dominance\_warning}: $88.3\%$ ($1190/1347$, CI$[87,90]$).
  \item \texttt{(none)} no-scaffold baseline: $85.6\%$ ($1153/1347$, CI$[84,87]$).
  \item \texttt{intent\_and\_register\_respect}: $79.9\%$ ($1076/1347$,
    CI$[78,82]$)---this scaffold \emph{underperforms the no-scaffold baseline}, so
    not all scaffolds help; scaffold choice matters.
  \item The selected $s^\star{=}$\texttt{interpretive\_direction} lifts abstention
    $+14.3$ points over the no-scaffold baseline ($99.9\%$ vs.\ $85.6\%$); the two
    intermediate scaffolds bracket the baseline (one above, one below). This backs
    the central contribution behaviorally and corroborates the representational
    finding (scaffold-vs-none silhouette $+0.48$, \Cref{app:geometry}) and the
    causal steering (\Cref{app:steer}).
\end{itemize}

\section{Divergence}
\label{app:divergence}

\paragraph{Methods.}
The divergence study scores each prompt at four readouts (3-opt, 2-opt, greedy-,
and sampled-thinking) under the per-condition gold. The sampled-thinking variant
draws $N$ generations $g_i \sim \LLM(x)$, parses each to $r_i$, and over the
$M \le N$ parsed draws forms
\begin{equation}
  p^{\mathrm{th}}_y \;=\; \frac{1}{M}\sum_{i=1}^{M} \mathbf{1}[\, r_i = y \,],
  \label{eq:pth}
\end{equation}
with prediction $\hat{y}^{\mathrm{th}} = \argmax_y p^{\mathrm{th}}_y$ (ties resolve
to $u$). To characterize the reasoning distribution we use Shannon entropy and
divergence from correct abstention,
\begin{equation}
  H(p) = -\sum_{y} p_y \log p_y,
  \qquad
  \mathrm{JS}\!\left(p^{\mathrm{th}} \,\|\, \delta_u\right),
  \label{eq:entropy-js}
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{JS}$ is the Jensen--Shannon divergence (low when free-form reasoning
concentrates on $u$).

\paragraph{Results.}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
  \item Scored set: $43$ items of $47$ prompts ($4$ unparseable thinking draws);
    $14$ ambiguous $+\,29$ disambiguated.
  \item Ambiguous accuracy by readout: sampled thinking $86\%$ ($12/14$);
    three-option $79\%$ ($11/14$); greedy-thinking $64\%$ ($9/14$); two-option
    undefined (no unknown option).
  \item Disambiguated accuracy by readout: two-option $59\%$ ($17/29$); sampled
    thinking $48\%$ ($14/29$); greedy-thinking $41\%$ ($12/29$); three-option
    $28\%$ ($8/29$).
  \item Ambiguous default thinking mix: $84\%$ unknown, $9\%$ target, $7\%$ other;
    $11$ of $14$ items sit at $[0,0,1]$ (JS-divergence $0$).
  \item Mean JS-divergence from $\delta_u$: $0.092$; three items diverge (one onto
    \emph{other} at $\mathrm{JS}=\ln 2 = 0.693$, two toward target at $0.216$ and
    $0.380$).
\end{itemize}

\section{Geometry}
\label{app:geometry}

\paragraph{Methods.}
Along the greedy reasoning path we capture the residual-stream activation
$h^{(l)}_p(x) \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$ at every layer $l$ from mid to last ($14$--$27$)
and at \emph{eight} structural token positions $p \in \{\,\text{im\_end},\
\text{newline},\ \text{im\_start},\ \text{assistant},\ \text{think\_open},\
\text{think\_close},\ \text{answer\_prefix},\ \text{label}\,\}$, under scaffold and
no-scaffold conditions ($n{=}462$: $231$ scaffold $+$ $231$ no-scaffold items). At
each layer and position we stack the per-sample vectors, fit a (mean-centering)
PCA, and colour points by each per-sample signal (context condition, accuracy, gold
label, question polarity, bias category, target/other identity). For every such
labelling we score the \emph{silhouette separability} in the full PCA space:
positive means same-label points cluster, near zero means no structure, negative
means same-label points are no closer than random.

\paragraph{Results.}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
  \item Last-layer answer-label silhouette separability is dominated by
    \emph{scaffold} presence ($+0.48$; $+0.35$ to $+0.60$ across positions,
    separable at every layer).
  \item Next is \emph{selected\_role} ($+0.20$, rising to $+0.36$ at mid-layer
    $21$).
  \item Near-zero axes: accuracy ($+0.04$), context\_condition ($+0.05$),
    gold\_role ($+0.05$), question\_polarity ($+0.01$).
  \item Anti-separable: target\_identity ($-0.47$), other\_identity ($-0.48$).
  \item Named conditions project onto a single arc (PC1 $68\%$ EV, PC2 $9\%$ EV);
    identity axes carry $152/154$ distinct identity strings ($n{=}462$), so
    anti-separability reflects near-unique identity labels, not clustering by
    social group.
\end{itemize}

\section{Steering: making the scaffold axis causal}
\label{app:steer}

\paragraph{Methods.}
\Cref{app:geometry} shows the \emph{scaffold} direction is the most separable axis
of the answer representation, but that is correlational. To test it causally we
turn the geometry into an additive intervention. From the geometry residuals
(\Cref{app:geometry}) we pair each item's scaffold and no-scaffold activation by
question id and take the per-layer \emph{diff-of-means}, $v^{(l)} =
\overline{h^{(l)}_{\text{scaffold}}} - \overline{h^{(l)}_{\text{none}}}$, as a
steering vector at the primary layer $l{=}14$ (depth $0.50$). We split the
$231$ pairs by question id into a seeded $70/30$ train/test split (no item
overlap) and \emph{fit nothing} beyond the train-mean direction; all numbers below
are on the held-out \textbf{test} items. At decode we add $\alpha\,v^{(14)}$ to the
\texttt{resid\_post} stream of every token and re-read the teacher-forced
three-option abstention on the $n{=}24$ ambiguous test items, sweeping
$\alpha \in [-2, 4]$. A positive $\alpha$ should \emph{raise} abstention (push
toward the scaffold); a negative $\alpha$ should suppress it.

\paragraph{Results.}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
  \item Adding the scaffold axis \emph{causally raises abstention} on held-out
    ambiguous items: test abstention climbs from $0.54$ at $\alpha{=}0$ to $0.71$
    at $\alpha{=}{+}1$ and a peak $0.75$ at $\alpha{=}{+}2$ (unknown-mass
    $0.49 \to 0.62$).
  \item The negative direction \emph{suppresses} it: $\alpha{=}{-}2$ drops
    abstention to $0.17$ (unknown-mass $0.18$), confirming a signed, monotone axis
    rather than a generic noise perturbation.
  \item The effect is bounded, not a runaway: over-steering at $\alpha{=}{+}4$
    collapses back to the $\alpha{=}0$ baseline ($0.54$), so the axis recovers part
    of---but does not saturate to---the scaffold ceiling.
  \item Reference ceiling: the actual scaffold prompt reaches $1.00$ abstention
    ($0.99$ unknown-mass) on the same $n{=}24$ test items, so a single mid-layer
    diff-of-means vector recovers a substantial fraction of the scaffold's
    behavioral effect with no prompt change.
\end{itemize}
