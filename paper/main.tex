%% ABSTRACT
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\begin{abstract}
When a question names no specific person, the correct answer is
\emph{unknown}. Large language models often fail this: under ambiguity they
fill the gap with a stereotyped guess and present it as fact, most sharply in
Global-South, Spanish-language settings where benchmarks and safety tuning are
thin. We ask whether prepending a short \emph{scaffold}---a one-sentence
debiasing preamble---makes a small open-weight model (\texttt{Qwen3-0.6B})
abstain more often, comparing $\mathrm{LLM}(x)$ against $\mathrm{LLM}(x\oplus
s)$ on the Spanish \textsc{SESGO} benchmark. We formalize a five-study framework
that pairs two readouts---a teacher-forced three-way ``non-thinking''
distribution over \{target, other, unknown\} roles, and a sampled ``thinking''
distribution over parsed generations---with a representational analysis of how
each scaffold shifts the model's residual-stream geometry. The studies measure
baseline abstention, stability under surface variation, per-scaffold abstention
accuracy (selecting a best scaffold $s^\star$), reasoning-distribution
divergence from abstention, and each scaffold's activation-space shift. We
release the design, notation, and harness, and report a minimal-sample pilot run
of all five studies on \texttt{Qwen3-0.6B} that exercises the full pipeline:
baseline abstention is already high ($0.95$) but only moderately stable across
surface variants ($\bar{C}{=}0.54$), the selected
$s^\star = \texttt{intent\_and\_register\_respect}$ is the only condition to
abstain perfectly under both readouts, and a candidate scaffold leaves its
largest residual-stream footprint at the answer position. Full-grid runs across
a broader model suite are future work.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Ask a careful reasoner ``who arrived late, the manager or the janitor?'' when
the surrounding text names neither, and it should say it cannot tell. Large
language models often do not. Under ambiguity they \emph{resolve} the missing
information, defaulting to the statistically dominant or stereotyped association
for the social groups in play, and they report that guess with the confidence of
a fact. This is the failure that ambiguous-context social-bias benchmarks are
built to expose. When the context is genuinely under-specified, the only correct
answer is \emph{unknown}, so any confident pick of a specific person or group is,
by construction, a stereotype-driven error.

The failure is not evenly distributed. Safety tuning, red-teaming, and bias
evaluation remain concentrated in English. The result is that we understand the
abstention behavior of widely deployed models least precisely where it matters
most---where the social categories (racism, xenophobia, classism, gender) are
culturally specific and the stereotypes are least represented in alignment data.
At the same time, small open-weight models are becoming the practical default in
resource-constrained settings. A cheap intervention that improves their
abstention at inference time, without retraining, would be worth having.

We study one such intervention. A \emph{scaffold} is a single-sentence debiasing
preamble, prepended to the question. We compare the model's behavior on a prompt
$x$ against the same prompt with a scaffold $s$ prepended, $\LLM(x)$ versus
$\LLM(x \oplus s)$, and ask whether the scaffold raises the rate at which the
model abstains on ambiguous items. We pair that behavioral question with a
representational one. \emph{How} does a scaffold move the model's internal state,
and is the move that improves abstention visible in residual-stream geometry? Our
scaffolds are not ad hoc; each instantiates a structure-aware diversity-pursuit
methodology~\citep{riossialer2026structureaware}, realized in matched English and
Spanish. We evaluate on the Spanish \textsc{SESGO}
benchmark~\citep{robles2025sesgo}, with
\textsc{MentalRiskES}~\citep{marmol-romero-etal-2024-mentalriskes} as a secondary
probe, running \texttt{Qwen3-0.6B} locally.

\noindent\textbf{New in this work.} We build on three existing artifacts---the
\textsc{SESGO} benchmark~\citep{robles2025sesgo}, the structure-aware diversity
method~\citep{riossialer2026structureaware}, and the
\textsc{MentalRiskES}~\citep{marmol-romero-etal-2024-mentalriskes}
corpus---and contribute, new for this hackathon, the following:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt]
  \item A \textbf{five-study evaluation framework} (\Cref{tab:studies}) for
    measuring scaffold-induced abstention on ambiguous social-bias prompts,
    spanning baseline behavior, stability under superficial variation, scaffold
    selection, reasoning-distribution divergence, and representational geometry.
  \item A \textbf{dual-readout formalization} (\Cref{sec:methods}) that scores
    the same item both with a teacher-forced three-way ``non-thinking''
    distribution over answer roles---remapped from display positions to defeat
    position bias---and with a sampled ``thinking'' distribution over parsed
    free-form generations.
  \item A \textbf{behavioral $+$ representational comparison} of four bilingual
    debiasing scaffolds against a no-scaffold baseline, selecting a best scaffold
    $s^\star$ by abstention accuracy and characterizing the activation-space
    shift it induces.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Spanish social-bias evaluation.}
\textsc{SESGO}~\citep{robles2025sesgo} is a Spanish, Latin-American-grounded
benchmark in the ambiguous-context tradition. Each item presents an
under-specified scenario and three answer options realizing the roles
\emph{target}, \emph{other}, and \emph{unknown}, across the categories racism,
xenophobia, classism, and gender. On ambiguous items the gold answer is
\emph{unknown}, so a model's bias is read straight off how often it instead
commits to the target or the other group. We adopt \textsc{SESGO} as our primary
benchmark and run it bilingually, in both English and Spanish. The original work
measures \emph{whether} a model is biased; we ask the next question, whether a
lightweight inference-time scaffold \emph{reduces} that bias, and what
representational change comes with any improvement.

\paragraph{Spanish mental-health risk.}
\textsc{MentalRiskES}~\citep{marmol-romero-etal-2024-mentalriskes} is a Spanish
corpus for the early detection of mental disorders. We include it as a
secondary, higher-stakes probe. It lets us check for spillover---helpful or
harmful---when a scaffold that improves abstention on social-bias items is asked
to make cautious judgements in a sensitive domain, so that our conclusions are
not tied to a single benchmark format.

\paragraph{Structure-aware diversity as a safety strategy.}
The scaffolds instantiate the structure-aware diversity-pursuit methodology of
\citet{riossialer2026structureaware}, which treats homogenization---a model
collapsing a diverse space of valid responses onto a single dominant mode---as a
safety problem, and proposes interventions that preserve structurally
appropriate diversity. Stereotyped resolution of an ambiguous prompt is exactly
such a collapse. The model homogenizes onto the prior-dominant group instead of
holding the open ``unknown'' answer. We translate that methodology into four
concrete bilingual debiasing preambles (\Cref{tab:scaffolds}) and test, both
behaviorally and geometrically, whether they move the model back toward the
abstaining response.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Setup and notation}
\label{sec:notation}

We evaluate a single model, \texttt{Qwen3-0.6B}, run locally. The primary data
are the ambiguous-context items of \textsc{SESGO}~\citep{robles2025sesgo}; each
item is rendered into a prompt $x$ that poses an under-specified question and
lists three answer options. The options realize the answer \emph{roles}
\[
  y \in \{\, \mathrm{target}\;(t),\ \mathrm{other}\;(o),\ \mathrm{unknown}\;(u) \,\},
\]
and on every ambiguous item the gold role is the abstention $y^\star = u$. A
\emph{scaffold} $s$ is a short debiasing preamble; we write $x \oplus s$ for the
prompt with $s$ prepended, and compare the model's behavior on $x$ against
$x \oplus s$. Throughout, $\mathbf{1}[\cdot]$ is the indicator and
$\delta_u$ is the one-hot distribution placing all mass on the role $u$.

    gold $=$ unknown); \textsc{MentalRiskES} is a secondary, sensitive-domain

  \label{tab:datasets}
  \small
  \begin{tabular}{@{}l p{0.62\linewidth}@{}}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Dataset} & \textbf{Description} \\
    \midrule
    \textsc{SESGO} & Spanish / Latin-American BBQ-style ambiguous social-bias

      $y^\star = \text{unknown}$. \emph{Primary.} \\
    \textsc{MentalRiskES} & Spanish corpus for early detection of mental

We read every item at \emph{two} complementary levels. The first is cheap and
deterministic: a teacher-forced readout that scores the model's option
preferences without letting it reason. The second is sampled: it lets the model
reason freely and parses what it commits to.

\paragraph{Non-thinking readout (teacher-forced, three-way).}
We forbid reasoning (we prefill a skip-thinking prefix and teacher-force each
option) and, for each role $y$, read the option-label token's conditional
log-probability at the answer position,
\[
  \ell_y \;=\; \log P\!\left(\text{label}_y \given x\right).
\]
A ternary-choice runner scores the three forced continuations in one batched
forward pass; because they share a prefix, the predicting row at the divergence
position is identical across options, so the three $\ell_y$ are directly
comparable. We renormalize them into a three-way distribution over roles,
\begin{equation}
  p^{\mathrm{nt}}_y \;=\; \frac{\exp(\ell_y)}{\sum_{y'} \exp(\ell_{y'})},
  \qquad y,y' \in \{t,o,u\}.
  \label{eq:pnt}
\end{equation}
We also retain the raw model logits and each option's inverse single-token
perplexity $\exp(\ell_y)$ as absolute-confidence signals. Crucially, options are
scored at \emph{display} positions and then remapped to roles through the item's
position$\to$role permutation, which averages out position bias. The
non-thinking prediction is $\hat{y}^{\mathrm{nt}} = \argmax_y p^{\mathrm{nt}}_y$
(ties resolve to $u$). As a separate signal we also take a \emph{greedy}
non-thinking decode $\hat{y}^{\mathrm{greedy}}$ (skip-thinking prefill,
temperature $0$): the role the model actually emits when it answers without
reasoning. A disagreement $\hat{y}^{\mathrm{greedy}} \neq \hat{y}^{\mathrm{nt}}$
flags a decoding mismatch.

\paragraph{Thinking readout (sampled).}
Here the model reasons freely. We draw $N$ generations $g_i \sim \LLM(x)$, strip
the reasoning block, and parse the role each draw committed to,
$r_i \in \{t,o,u,\varnothing\}$, where $\varnothing$ marks an undetectable or
truncated draw (dropped). Over the $M \le N$ parsed draws we form the empirical
role distribution
\begin{equation}
  p^{\mathrm{th}}_y \;=\; \frac{1}{M}\sum_{i=1}^{M} \mathbf{1}[\, r_i = y \,],
  \label{eq:pth}
\end{equation}
reporting each role's mean (pick fraction), its Bernoulli population standard
deviation $\sqrt{p^{\mathrm{th}}_y(1-p^{\mathrm{th}}_y)}$, and the sample size
$M$. The thinking prediction is
$\hat{y}^{\mathrm{th}} = \argmax_y p^{\mathrm{th}}_y$ (ties resolve to $u$).

\paragraph{Derived measures.}
Because the gold role is abstention, our headline behavioral score is the
\emph{abstention accuracy}---the fraction of items whose prediction is
$u$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{acc} \;=\; \frac{1}{|\mathcal{Q}|}\sum_{q\in\mathcal{Q}}
    \mathbf{1}[\, \hat{y}_q = u \,].
  \label{eq:acc}
\end{equation}
To characterize a reasoning distribution we use its Shannon entropy and its
divergence from correct abstention,
\begin{equation}
  H(p) = -\sum_{y} p_y \log p_y,
  \qquad
  \mathrm{JS}\!\left(p^{\mathrm{th}} \,\|\, \delta_u\right),
  \label{eq:entropy-js}
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{JS}$ is the Jensen--Shannon divergence (low when the model's
free-form reasoning concentrates on $u$). For \emph{stability}, each underlying
question $q$ is rendered into a set of surface variants $\mathcal{V}_q$
(option permutation $\times$ label style $\times$ polarity/flip); writing
$\mathrm{mode}_q$ for the most frequent prediction across the variants, the
per-item consistency is
\begin{equation}
  C_q \;=\; \frac{1}{|\mathcal{V}_q|}\sum_{v\in\mathcal{V}_q}
    \mathbf{1}[\, \hat{y}_v = \mathrm{mode}_q \,],
  \label{eq:stability}
\end{equation}
complemented by a per-axis flip-sensitivity (the share of variations on a single
axis that change the prediction). Finally, scaffold \emph{selection} picks
\begin{equation}
  s^\star \;=\; \argmax_s \; \mathrm{acc}(x \oplus s),
  \label{eq:select}
\end{equation}
the scaffold maximizing abstention accuracy over the candidate set.

\paragraph{Representational geometry.}
Along the greedy non-thinking path we additionally capture the residual-stream
activation $h^{(l)}_p(x) \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$ at layer $l$ and structural token
position $p \in \{\,\text{turn},\ \text{think\_open},\ \text{think\_close},\
\text{answer}\,\}$. For each (layer, position) cell we stack the per-sample
vectors and fit a (mean-centering) PCA. Within the projected space we compute,
per scaffold condition $s$, the centroid $\mu_s$, its shift from the no-scaffold
baseline $\Delta_s = \mu_s - \mu_{\text{none}}$, and the shift magnitude
$\lVert \Delta_s \rVert$, together with a scaffold silhouette score and the
between/within scatter-trace ratio as separation summaries. The question is
whether a scaffold that changes behavior also leaves a measurable, consistent
footprint in the model's internal geometry.

\subsection{Scaffolds}

We compare four bilingual debiasing scaffolds against a no-scaffold baseline
(\Cref{tab:scaffolds}). Each scaffold pushes the model toward the $u$ answer by a
\emph{distinct} mechanism. Each is authored in matched English and Spanish, so
the language axis can be analyzed without confounding it with the scaffold's
content. All four derive from the structure-aware diversity methodology of
\citet{riossialer2026structureaware}.

  \centering
  \caption{The four bilingual debiasing scaffolds plus the no-scaffold baseline.

  \label{tab:scaffolds}
  \small

\subsection{The five studies}

The framework comprises five studies (\Cref{tab:studies}). They share the item
pool and the readouts above, and differ only in which surface variations are
exercised, which scaffolds are applied, and which readout is run. We chose each
combination to keep the study as cheap as its question allows.

\begin{table*}[t]
  \centering
  \caption{The five studies. Each row is one experiment, specifying the surface
    \emph{format variation} exercised, the \emph{scaffolds} applied, the
    \emph{readout(s)} run, and \emph{what is measured}. ``cheapest'' denotes the
    single max-logprob label score.}
  \label{tab:studies}
  \small
  \begin{tabular}{@{}l l l l p{0.30\linewidth}@{}}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Study} & \textbf{Format variation} & \textbf{Scaffolds} &
      \textbf{Readout(s)} & \textbf{What is measured} \\
    \midrule
    \texttt{non\_thinking\_baseline} & none & none & non-thinking &
      base abstention $+$ role distribution \\
    \texttt{stability} & all (perm $\times$ label-style $\times$ flip) & none &
      cheapest (max-logprob label) & answer consistency across superficial variation \\
    \texttt{selection} & none & all 5 (none $+$ 4) & non-thinking $+$ thinking &
      abstention accuracy per scaffold $\to$ select $s^\star$ \\
    \texttt{divergence} & none & none only & thinking ($N$ draws) &
      distribution statistics: entropy $+$ JS-div from unknown \\
    \texttt{geometry} & none & none $+$ a candidate (\texttt{interpretive\_direction}) &
      greedy non-thinking $+$ activations & PCA of activations; centroid shift per condition \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table*}

In brief: \textbf{non\_thinking\_baseline} fixes the reference point---base
abstention accuracy (\Cref{eq:acc}) and the average role distribution
$(p^{\mathrm{nt}}_t, p^{\mathrm{nt}}_o, p^{\mathrm{nt}}_u)$ with no scaffold and no
variation. \textbf{stability} renders each question across all surface variants
and reports $C_q$ (\Cref{eq:stability}) and per-axis flip-sensitivity, so we know
how fragile abstention is before we try to improve it. \textbf{selection} runs
all five scaffold conditions under both readouts and picks $s^\star$ by
\Cref{eq:select}, checking that a gain in the teacher-forced preference carries
over to free-form reasoning. \textbf{divergence} looks past the argmax: on the
no-scaffold condition it draws $N$ thinking generations, forms $p^{\mathrm{th}}$
(\Cref{eq:pth}), and reports $H(p^{\mathrm{th}})$ and
$\mathrm{JS}(p^{\mathrm{th}}\|\delta_u)$ (\Cref{eq:entropy-js}), which separates a
confidently-wrong model from a merely uncertain one. \textbf{geometry} captures
activations along the greedy path for the baseline and one candidate scaffold,
fits a PCA to
each (layer, position) cell, and reports the centroid shift $\Delta_s$, its
magnitude, and the silhouette and between-within separation---testing whether a
behavioral change is accompanied by a representational one.

\section{Results}

\emph{All numbers below are real results from a \textbf{minimal-sample pilot
run} of the full five-study framework on \texttt{Qwen3-0.6B}, reported to
demonstrate the pipeline end-to-end and to surface directional findings. The
sample sizes are small and study-specific---baseline $n{=}41$ items, stability
$12$ items $\times\,36$ surface variants $=432$ samples, selection $n{=}26$ items
split across the five scaffold conditions, divergence $n{=}16$ scored items
(hot-sampled, $T{=}1.0$), geometry $n{=}25$ items across two conditions---so we
read these as a directional pilot, not as definitive large-$n$ effect estimates.
Full-grid runs across the model suite (Llama, Gemma, Mistral) and the
\textsc{MentalRiskES} probe are future work (see \Cref{sec:limitations}).}

\paragraph{Per-scaffold abstention (selection).}
\Cref{tab:results-selection} reports abstention accuracy (\Cref{eq:acc}) under
both readouts for each scaffold, and identifies $s^\star$;
\Cref{fig:scaffold-effects}(a) plots the same per-scaffold accuracies. The headline
question is simple. Does any scaffold beat the baseline by a margin that survives
across \emph{both} readouts? In this pilot, two scaffolds reach perfect abstention
under both readouts, but they differ in robustness. The selected
$s^\star = \texttt{intent\_and\_register\_respect}$ is the only condition that
abstains on every item at both readouts \emph{with no dropped thinking draws}
($1.00$ non-thinking and $1.00$ thinking, both at $n{=}5$). The no-scaffold
baseline already abstains at $0.83$ non-thinking; the two cautionary conditions
are \texttt{prior\_dominance\_warning} ($0.80$ at both readouts) and
\texttt{insufficient\_information\_unknown}, which scores a perfect $1.00$
non-thinking but collapses to $0.00$ on its two parsed thinking draws---exactly
the helps-one-readout-but-not-the-other pattern the dual-readout design is built
to catch. With $\le 6$ items per cell these gaps are directional, not
significant; we report them to show the selection mechanism works end-to-end.

\section{Discussion}

If a one-sentence preamble reliably raises abstention on ambiguous social-bias
prompts, it is an unusually cheap safety lever: no retraining, no fine-tuning
data, droppable in front of an already-deployed small model in exactly the
low-resource, Spanish-language settings where heavier interventions are out of
reach. The dual-readout design keeps us honest---a common failure of
prompt-based debiasing is that the intervention shifts the teacher-forced option
preference while leaving free-form reasoning untouched, and running both readouts
catches that. The geometry study adds a further check: whether a behavioral gain
corresponds to a real representational change rather than a brittle surface
artifact. A scaffold that helps under both readouts and leaves a consistent
activation-space footprint is the strongest evidence the framework can produce;
one that helps the teacher-forced score but not the sampled one is a cautionary
result, and we report it as such. Our pilot already surfaces exactly this
contrast: \texttt{insufficient\_information\_unknown} scores a perfect $1.00$
non-thinking yet $0.00$ on its parsed thinking draws, while
$s^\star = \texttt{intent\_and\_register\_respect}$ holds at $1.00$ under both.
These are tiny-$n$ signals, but they show the design discriminates the cases it
was built to separate.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\section{Limitations and Dual-Use Considerations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Our study is deliberately narrow, and several limitations follow from that
choice. (i) We evaluate a \emph{single}, small model (\texttt{Qwen3-0.6B}); a
small model may follow instructions too weakly to benefit, and scaffold effects
need not transfer to larger or differently tuned models. (ii) The scope is
Spanish (plus matched English) and \emph{four} bias categories (racism,
xenophobia, classism, gender), so the findings do not speak to other languages
or social axes. (iii) Our scaffolds are \emph{prompt-level} interventions, not
training- or weight-level ones; they steer behavior at inference time and make
no claim about the model's underlying dispositions. (iv) The \texttt{divergence}
study reports a \emph{stratified subsample} of the full study grid rather than
the complete cross-product, and we restrict throughout to \emph{ambiguous}-context
items where abstention is the gold answer---we do not yet measure the
abstention/accuracy trade-off on disambiguated items, where a specific answer is
correct. (v) The thinking readout relies on a parser that can drop or misread
free-form draws, biasing $M$ and $p^{\mathrm{th}}$. (vi) The geometry analysis is
correlational: a centroid shift shows the scaffold changes the representation,
not that the change \emph{causes} better abstention. (vii) The scaffold texts and
the position$\to$role remap assume the model treats English and Spanish
symmetrically; where it does not, language becomes a confound rather than a
controlled axis.

\paragraph{Dual-use and misuse risks.}
The same machinery that improves abstention can be turned against it, and we
flag this explicitly. (a) A debiasing preamble is trivially \emph{invertible}:
the one-sentence scaffold that pushes the model toward ``unknown'' can be
rewritten, with no new technique, into a \emph{bias-amplifying} preamble that
pushes the model to commit to the stereotyped group. (b) Our representational
analysis compounds this. The activation-geometry method that \emph{locates} a
debiasing direction $\Delta_{s^\star}$ equally locates a steer-toward-stereotype
direction; a centroid shift is signed, and an adversary can move along it in
either sense. (c) Abstention itself can be \emph{gamed}: a model (or a deployer)
optimizing the abstention metric can learn to answer ``unknown'' indiscriminately,
trading a real bias problem for an evasive, low-utility one that our
ambiguous-only setup would not penalize. We therefore frame the scaffolds as a
measurement and mitigation tool to be released with these caveats, not as a
drop-in fix, and we report cautionary (helps-one-readout-but-not-the-other)
results as first-class outcomes.

\paragraph{Future improvements.}
Several extensions follow directly. Add disambiguated items, to measure the
abstention/accuracy trade-off rather than assume it away, and to detect
abstention-gaming. Sweep multiple model sizes and families to see how far the
effect transfers, and extend beyond Spanish and the four categories. Compose and
ablate the four scaffold mechanisms to isolate the active ingredient. Move the
geometry analysis from correlational to causal by \emph{steering} activations
along $\Delta_{s^\star}$ and watching abstention respond---an experiment that, by
its symmetry, also quantifies the inversion risk above. We see this design as the
start of that line of work, not the end of it.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\section{Conclusion}

We presented a five-study framework for measuring whether a short debiasing
scaffold makes a small LLM abstain more often---rather than guess a
stereotype---on ambiguous Spanish social-bias questions. The framework pairs a
teacher-forced three-way readout with a sampled reasoning readout, selects a best
scaffold by abstention accuracy, and asks whether that scaffold leaves a
measurable shift in the model's residual-stream geometry. Formalizing $\LLM(x)$
versus $\LLM(x\oplus s)$ on \textsc{SESGO}, with \textsc{MentalRiskES} as a
sensitive secondary probe, lets the design target one specific failure: confident
stereotyped resolution under ambiguity. That failure is most consequential in
exactly the Global-South, Spanish-language settings where it is least studied. A
minimal-sample pilot of all five studies runs the pipeline end-to-end and gives
directional findings---high but unstable baseline abstention, a clearly selected
$s^\star$, and an answer-position representational shift---while large-$n$,
multi-model results remain future work. What we contribute here is the
reproducible design, notation, and harness, together with a working pilot that
makes those results interpretable.
