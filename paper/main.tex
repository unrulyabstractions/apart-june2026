%% ABSTRACT
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\begin{abstract}
On ambiguous questions naming no specific person the correct answer is
\emph{unknown}; on disambiguated ones a specific role is correct. We audit this
abstain-or-guess behavior on the Spanish \textsc{SESGO} benchmark with a
five-study framework that scores every item under three readouts---a
teacher-forced three-option distribution over \{target, other, unknown\}, a
two-option forced choice, and a greedy reasoning decode---against a per-condition
gold. On \texttt{Qwen3-0.6B} ($n{=}2310$, four bias categories) the model
abstains well above chance under teacher forcing (71--87\%) but commits poorly
once a role is required (28--46\%), leaking asymmetric mass onto the non-target
group. A five-model Qwen/Llama/Mistral sweep shows abstention and disambiguated
accuracy both rise monotonically with scale; the smallest substitutes confident
stereotyped guessing for abstention. Probing answer-position activations
($n{=}462$), the representation separates by context condition but collapses on
social identity and bias category. We release the design and harness.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Ask a careful reasoner ``who arrived late, the manager or the janitor?'' when
the surrounding text names neither, and it should say it cannot tell. Large
language models often do not. Under ambiguity they \emph{resolve} the missing
information, defaulting to the statistically dominant or stereotyped association
for the social groups in play, and they report that guess with the confidence of
a fact. This is the failure that ambiguous-context social-bias benchmarks are
built to expose. When the context is genuinely under-specified, the only correct
answer is \emph{unknown}, so any confident pick of a specific person or group is,
by construction, a stereotype-driven error. But abstention is only half of the
behavior worth measuring. The same benchmark also presents \emph{disambiguated}
items, where the text does name a specific person, and there the correct move
flips: the model must commit to the labelled role rather than hide behind
``unknown.'' A model is well-behaved only if it does \emph{both}---abstains when
it should and answers when it should---so we measure the two conditions side by
side, under a \emph{per-condition} gold (ambiguous: gold $=$ abstain/unknown;
disambiguated: gold $=$ the labelled role).

The failure is not evenly distributed. Safety tuning, red-teaming, and bias
evaluation remain concentrated in English. The result is that we understand the
abstention behavior of widely deployed models least precisely where it matters
most---where the social categories (racism, xenophobia, classism, gender) are
culturally specific and the stereotypes are least represented in alignment data.
At the same time, small open-weight models are becoming the practical default in
resource-constrained settings, so it is precisely these models, in precisely
these settings, whose abstain-or-guess behavior we most need to characterize.

We do so with a five-study framework, anchored on the Spanish \textsc{SESGO}
benchmark~\citep{robles2025sesgo} and running open-weight instruction-tuned
models locally. We score each item under \emph{three} readouts---a teacher-forced
three-option distribution over the \{target, other, unknown\} roles, a two-option
forced choice with the unknown option removed, and a greedy reasoning decode---and
read all three against the per-condition gold. We then ask how the behavior scales:
a cross-model sweep over five Qwen, Llama, and Mistral models spanning
0.6--7.25\,B parameters. Finally we pair the behavioral picture with a
representational one---probing answer-position residual-stream activations to ask
\emph{what} the model encodes about an item when it answers. A short bilingual
debiasing \emph{scaffold} (one-sentence preamble, comparing $\LLM(x)$ against
$\LLM(x \oplus s)$), instantiating a structure-aware diversity-pursuit
methodology~\citep{riossialer2026structureaware}, supplies the
controlled-intervention axis used in two of the studies.

\noindent\textbf{New in this work.} We build on two existing artifacts---the
\textsc{SESGO} benchmark~\citep{robles2025sesgo} and the structure-aware diversity
method~\citep{riossialer2026structureaware}---and contribute, new for this
hackathon, the following:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt]
  \item A \textbf{five-study evaluation framework} (\Cref{tab:studies}) for
    auditing abstain-or-guess behavior on ambiguous \emph{and} disambiguated
    social-bias prompts, spanning baseline behavior, stability under superficial
    variation, scaffold selection, reasoning-distribution divergence, and
    representational geometry.
  \item A \textbf{multi-readout, per-condition formalization} (\Cref{sec:methods})
    that scores the same item under a teacher-forced three-option ``non-thinking''
    distribution (remapped from display positions to defeat position bias), a
    two-option forced choice, and a greedy reasoning decode, each against a
    per-condition gold.
  \item A \textbf{cross-model size sweep} (\Cref{sec:cross-model}) across five
    Qwen/Llama/Mistral instruction-tuned models, showing how both abstention and
    disambiguated accuracy scale with model size.
  \item A \textbf{behavioral $+$ representational comparison}: probing
    answer-position activations to show that the geometry separates by context
    condition but collapses on social identity and bias category.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Spanish social-bias evaluation.}
\textsc{SESGO}~\citep{robles2025sesgo} is a Spanish, Latin-American-grounded
benchmark in the BBQ ambiguous/disambiguated tradition. Each item presents a
scenario and three answer options realizing the roles \emph{target},
\emph{other}, and \emph{unknown}, across the categories racism, xenophobia,
classism, and gender. On \emph{ambiguous} items the text names no one and the
gold answer is \emph{unknown}, so a model's bias is read off how often it instead
commits to the target or the other group; on \emph{disambiguated} items the text
does name a person, and the gold answer is that labelled role. We adopt
\textsc{SESGO} as our primary benchmark. The original work measures
\emph{whether} a model is biased on this benchmark; we ask how that behavior looks
when the same item is read under several escalating readouts, how it scales across
model families and sizes, and what the model's internal representation encodes
about the item when it answers.

\paragraph{Structure-aware diversity as a safety strategy.}
Two of our studies use a bilingual debiasing \emph{scaffold} as a controlled
intervention. The scaffolds instantiate the structure-aware diversity-pursuit
methodology of \citet{riossialer2026structureaware}, which treats
homogenization---a model collapsing a diverse space of valid responses onto a
single dominant mode---as a safety problem, and proposes interventions that
preserve structurally appropriate diversity. Stereotyped resolution of an
ambiguous prompt is exactly such a collapse: the model homogenizes onto the
prior-dominant group instead of holding the open ``unknown'' answer. We translate
that methodology into four concrete bilingual debiasing preambles
(\Cref{tab:scaffolds}) and use them as the intervention axis in the selection and
geometry studies.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Setup and notation}
\label{sec:notation}

Our primary subject is \texttt{Qwen3-0.6B}, run locally; the cross-model study
(\Cref{sec:cross-model}) extends to four further models. The data are the
\textsc{SESGO} items~\citep{robles2025sesgo}; each item is rendered into a prompt
$x$ that poses a question and lists answer options realizing the \emph{roles}
\[
  y \in \{\, \mathrm{target}\;(t),\ \mathrm{other}\;(o),\ \mathrm{unknown}\;(u) \,\}.
\]
The gold role $y^\star$ is set \emph{per condition}: on \emph{ambiguous} items the
text names no one, so $y^\star = u$ (abstain); on \emph{disambiguated} items the
text names a person, so $y^\star$ is the labelled role ($t$ or $o$). A
\emph{scaffold} $s$ is a short debiasing preamble; we write $x \oplus s$ for the
prompt with $s$ prepended. Throughout, $\mathbf{1}[\cdot]$ is the indicator and
$\delta_u$ is the one-hot distribution placing all mass on $u$. The items span
the categories racism, xenophobia, classism, and gender.

We read every item at \emph{three} complementary levels. The first two are cheap
and deterministic: teacher-forced readouts that score the model's option
preferences without letting it reason---once over all three roles, and once over
just the two named roles ($t,o$) with the unknown option removed. The third is a
greedy reasoning decode that lets the model think and parses what it commits to;
a sampled variant of it drives the divergence study.

\paragraph{Teacher-forced readouts (three-option and two-option).}
We forbid reasoning (we prefill a skip-thinking prefix and teacher-force each
option) and, for each role $y$, read the option-label token's conditional
log-probability at the answer position,
\[
  \ell_y \;=\; \log P\!\left(\text{label}_y \given x\right).
\]
A choice runner scores the forced continuations in one batched forward pass;
because they share a prefix, the predicting row at the divergence position is
identical across options, so the $\ell_y$ are directly comparable. The
\emph{three-option} readout renormalizes over all three roles,
\begin{equation}
  p^{\mathrm{nt}}_y \;=\; \frac{\exp(\ell_y)}{\sum_{y'} \exp(\ell_{y'})},
  \qquad y,y' \in \{t,o,u\},
  \label{eq:pnt}
\end{equation}
with chance $1/3$; the \emph{two-option} forced-choice readout drops the unknown
option and renormalizes over $\{t,o\}$ only (chance $1/2$), so it cannot abstain
and is undefined ($N/A$) on ambiguous items. Crucially, options are scored at
\emph{display} positions and then remapped to roles through the item's
position$\to$role permutation, which averages out position bias. The prediction
is $\hat{y} = \argmax_y p_y$ (ties resolve to $u$ in the three-option case).

\paragraph{Greedy-thinking readout (decoded).}
The third readout lets the model reason. We take a greedy reasoning decode
(temperature $0$), strip the reasoning block, and parse the role the model
committed to, $r \in \{t,o,u,\varnothing\}$, where $\varnothing$ marks an
undetectable or truncated draw (dropped). Its chance level is $1/3$. The
\textbf{divergence} study uses a \emph{sampled} variant: we draw $N$ generations
$g_i \sim \LLM(x)$, parse each to $r_i$, and over the $M \le N$ parsed draws form
the empirical role distribution
\begin{equation}
  p^{\mathrm{th}}_y \;=\; \frac{1}{M}\sum_{i=1}^{M} \mathbf{1}[\, r_i = y \,],
  \label{eq:pth}
\end{equation}
reporting each role's pick fraction, its Bernoulli standard deviation
$\sqrt{p^{\mathrm{th}}_y(1-p^{\mathrm{th}}_y)}$, and the sample size $M$. The
sampled-thinking prediction is $\hat{y}^{\mathrm{th}} = \argmax_y p^{\mathrm{th}}_y$
(ties resolve to $u$).

\paragraph{Derived measures.}
Our headline behavioral score is \emph{per-condition accuracy}---the fraction of
items whose prediction matches the condition's gold role $y^\star_q$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{acc} \;=\; \frac{1}{|\mathcal{Q}|}\sum_{q\in\mathcal{Q}}
    \mathbf{1}[\, \hat{y}_q = y^\star_q \,].
  \label{eq:acc}
\end{equation}
On ambiguous items ($y^\star_q = u$) this reduces to \emph{abstention accuracy};
on disambiguated items it is the rate of naming the correct labelled role, and
the $t$-vs-$o$ accuracy gap within disambiguated items is the bias signal. We
report Wilson 95\% confidence intervals on every rate. The three-option role
masses $(p^{\mathrm{nt}}_t,p^{\mathrm{nt}}_o,p^{\mathrm{nt}}_u)$ give a companion,
softer view of where probability leaks.
To characterize a reasoning distribution we use its Shannon entropy and its
divergence from correct abstention,
\begin{equation}
  H(p) = -\sum_{y} p_y \log p_y,
  \qquad
  \mathrm{JS}\!\left(p^{\mathrm{th}} \,\|\, \delta_u\right),
  \label{eq:entropy-js}
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{JS}$ is the Jensen--Shannon divergence (low when the model's
free-form reasoning concentrates on $u$). For \emph{stability}, each item is
rendered into a set of $18$ purely cosmetic surface variants $\mathcal{V}_q$
($3$ label styles $\times$ $6$ role permutations, gold held fixed); writing
$\mathrm{mode}_q$ for the modal prediction across the variants, the per-item
consistency is
\begin{equation}
  C_q \;=\; \frac{1}{|\mathcal{V}_q|}\sum_{v\in\mathcal{V}_q}
    \mathbf{1}[\, \hat{y}_v = \mathrm{mode}_q \,],
  \label{eq:stability}
\end{equation}
complemented by a per-axis format-sensitivity (the share of variations on a
single axis that change the prediction). Finally, scaffold \emph{selection} picks
\begin{equation}
  s^\star \;=\; \argmax_s \; \mathrm{acc}(x \oplus s),
  \label{eq:select}
\end{equation}
the scaffold maximizing abstention accuracy over the candidate set.

\paragraph{Representational geometry.}
Along the greedy reasoning path we additionally capture the residual-stream
activation $h^{(l)}_p(x) \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$ at layer $l$ and structural token
position $p \in \{\,\text{turn},\ \text{think\_open},\ \text{think\_close},\
\text{answer}\,\}$. We focus on the \emph{answer} position with $l$ layer-averaged,
stack the per-sample vectors, and fit a (mean-centering) PCA. To ask \emph{what}
the answer representation encodes, we score, for every candidate labelling of the
items (context condition, accuracy, gold label, question polarity, bias category,
target/other identity), the \emph{silhouette separability} of that labelling in
the full PCA space: a positive silhouette means points sharing a label cluster
together, a value near zero means no structure, and a negative value means
same-label points are no closer than random. The question is which axes of the
data the model's internal state actually organizes by when it answers.

The framework comprises five studies (\Cref{tab:studies}). They share the item
pool and the readouts above, and differ only in which surface variations are
exercised, which scaffolds are applied, and which readout is run. We chose each
combination to keep the study as cheap as its question allows.

In brief: \textbf{baseline} fixes the reference point---per-condition accuracy
(\Cref{eq:acc}) under all three readouts and the average role distribution
$(p^{\mathrm{nt}}_t, p^{\mathrm{nt}}_o, p^{\mathrm{nt}}_u)$, sliced by bias
category. \textbf{cross\_model} repeats the baseline readouts across five
instruction-tuned models and plots accuracy against log model size.
\textbf{stability} renders each item across $18$ cosmetic surface variants and
reports $C_q$ (\Cref{eq:stability}) and per-axis format-sensitivity, so we know
how fragile each behavior is. \textbf{selection} runs all five scaffold conditions
and picks $s^\star$ by \Cref{eq:select}, checking that a gain in the teacher-forced
preference carries over to free-form reasoning. \textbf{divergence} looks past the
argmax: it draws $N$ thinking generations, forms $p^{\mathrm{th}}$ (\Cref{eq:pth}),
and reports per-readout accuracy and $\mathrm{JS}(p^{\mathrm{th}}\|\delta_u)$
(\Cref{eq:entropy-js}), separating a confidently-wrong model from a merely
uncertain one. \textbf{geometry} captures answer-position activations and reports
the silhouette separability of the representation by every item label, testing
\emph{what} the model encodes about an item when it answers.

\section{Results}

All numbers below are real run values, cross-checked against the raw
\texttt{response\_samples.json} via the project's own slicing code. We report
per-condition accuracy with Wilson $95\%$ confidence intervals; $n$'s are
study-specific and stated inline. The headline baseline is the full SESGO grid on
\texttt{Qwen3-0.6B} ($n{=}2310$ scored items: $1155$ question\,ids
$\times\,2$ context conditions, across four bias categories), and the headline
scaling result is a five-model size sweep.

\subsection{Baseline: abstain well, commit poorly, asymmetrically}
\label{sec:baseline}

\Cref{fig:baseline} reads the full grid under all three readouts, sliced by bias
category. The picture is consistent. On \emph{ambiguous} items (gold $=$ abstain)
the model abstains well above the $1/3$ chance line under three-option teacher
forcing---$87\%$ clasismo ($n{=}136$, CI$[80,91]$), $81\%$ racismo ($n{=}240$,
CI$[76,86]$), $71\%$ xenofobia ($n{=}388$, CI$[67,76]$), and a (severely
underpowered) $100\%$ genero ($n{=}6$). But on \emph{disambiguated} items (gold
$=$ the labelled role) accuracy is low and \emph{asymmetric}, and that
target-vs-other gap is the bias signal. Under three-option forcing, clasismo
answers the target-gold role more accurately than the other-gold role
($46\%$ vs.\ $35\%$, both $n{=}136$), racismo \emph{reverses}
($28\%$ target vs.\ $43\%$ other, $n{=}240$), and xenofobia is roughly symmetric
($44\%$ vs.\ $40\%$, $n{=}388$). The two-option forced choice (no unknown option,
chance $1/2$) lifts disambiguated accuracy toward/above chance but preserves the
asymmetry (racismo $50\%$ target vs.\ $64\%$ other; xenofobia $58\%$ vs.\ $62\%$;
both $n{=}240/388$). The companion role-probability mass (\Cref{fig:baseline}(b))
corroborates it: unknown carries the plurality of mass in every category
(clasismo $0.536$, racismo $0.477$, xenofobia $0.432$, genero $0.412$), and within
the named roles the model leaks asymmetric mass onto \emph{other} over
\emph{target} in racismo ($0.322$ vs.\ $0.202$) and genero ($0.362$ vs.\ $0.226$)

below chance (racismo $49\%$, xenofobia $45\%$) while holding for clasismo
($85\%$)---reasoning can talk the model out of abstaining. The genero category
($n{=}18$ total) is too small to read.

\subsection{Cross-model size sweep: both behaviors scale}
\label{sec:cross-model}

Our headline scaling result places five instruction-tuned models---Qwen3
(0.6\,B, 4\,B), Llama-3.2 (1\,B, 3\,B), and Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3---against log
model size, with $n{=}770$ per metric cell per model (\Cref{fig:size-sweep}). The
dominant, consistent trend is that \textbf{abstention rises with scale within
every family}: on the three-option readout Qwen climbs $0.774$ (0.6\,B)
$\to 0.892$ (4\,B) and Llama jumps $0.299$ (1\,B) $\to 0.887$ (3\,B); the same
monotone rise holds for greedy-thinking abstention (Qwen $0.534 \to 0.718$; Llama
$0.275 \to 0.834$). The smallest \texttt{Llama-3.2-1B} is the clear outlier:
it abstains least of any model on the three-option readout ($0.299$, below the
$1/3$ chance line) and instead far more often commits to the target identity
(disambiguated-target $0.678$ vs.\ $0.395$ for \texttt{Qwen3-0.6B})---small models
default to guessing the stereotyped role rather than saying ``unknown.''
\textbf{Disambiguated accuracy scales too}: on the two-option forced choice
(no unknown option, so abstention is $0$ by construction) average disambiguated
accuracy climbs from $\sim\!0.59$ for the sub-1.3\,B models
($0.596$ Qwen-0.6B, $0.587$ Llama-1B) to $0.82$--$0.88$ for the 3--7\,B models
($0.822$ Llama-3B, $0.882$ Qwen-4B, $0.835$ Mistral-7B). Net: both the willingness
to abstain under ambiguity and the ability to identify the correct role under
disambiguation improve with scale, and the very smallest models substitute
confident stereotyped guessing for abstention.

\subsection{Stability: ambiguous abstention is fragile under cosmetic variation}
\label{sec:stability}

\Cref{fig:stab-div}(a) probes how robust each behavior is to pure surface variation
($18$ cosmetic renderings per item: $3$ label styles $\times\,6$ role
permutations, gold fixed; single model, racismo, Spanish). Under the three-option
readout, per-condition accuracy is only $44\%$ on ambiguous items
($63/144$, CI$[36,52]$)---barely above the $1/3$ line---versus $57\%$ on
disambiguated items ($163/288$, CI$[51,62]$); the two-option readout (N/A on
ambiguous items) reaches $60\%$ disambiguated ($174/288$, CI$[55,66]$). Per-item
consistency $C_q$ (\Cref{eq:stability}) shows the same gap: ambiguous items keep
their modal three-option answer in only $51\%\pm4\%$ of variations ($n{=}8$
items), well below the $>\!0.8$ ``robust'' zone, whereas disambiguated items are
stable at $86\%\pm4\%$ ($n{=}16$). Collapsing to the binary two-option choice
raises consistency on both (ambiguous $83\%\pm6\%$, disambiguated $90\%\pm4\%$),
indicating much of the three-option instability on ambiguous items is the model
thrashing among target/other/unknown rather than committing to abstention.

\subsection{Selection: a debiasing scaffold and the dual-readout check}
\label{sec:selection}

The selection run evaluates the baseline scaffold on $35$ ambiguous items
(gold $=$ unknown), so here per-condition accuracy reduces to abstention accuracy
(\Cref{fig:stab-div}(b)). The three-option non-thinking readout abstains correctly
on $80\%$ of items ($28/35$, CI$[64,90]$), and the sampled thinking readout---run
on the $22$-item subset that produced parsable thinking draws---abstains on $86\%$
($19/22$, CI$[67,95]$), so thinking modestly improves abstention on the matched
subset (the selected $s^\star$, gold star, is the baseline itself, the only
scaffold run). Within the thinking readout, abstention is perfect for the smaller
categories (clasismo $100\%$, $n{=}3$; racismo $100\%$, $n{=}6$) and lowest for
xenofobia ($77\%$, $10/13$): the residual non-abstention is concentrated in
xenofobia. The two-option forced choice abstains $0\%$ by construction (it can
only pick target$=14$ vs.\ other$=21$).

\subsection{Divergence: the readout that protects abstention cripples role-calling}
\label{sec:divergence}

Scoring each prompt at four escalating readouts under the per-condition gold
exposes a clean inversion (\Cref{fig:divergence-acc}). On the $14$ \emph{ambiguous}
items, where abstaining is correct, sampled thinking is best ($86\%$, $12/14$),
three-option teacher forcing follows ($79\%$, $11/14$), and deterministic
greedy-thinking is worst ($64\%$, $9/14$); the two-option forced choice is
undefined (no unknown option). The picture \emph{inverts} on the $29$
\emph{disambiguated} items, where a role must be named: the two-option forced
choice leads ($59\%$, $17/29$), sampled thinking reaches only $48\%$ ($14/29$),
greedy-thinking $41\%$ ($12/29$), and the three-option readout collapses to $28\%$
($8/29$)---its strong abstention bias becomes a liability once a role is called
for. On the ambiguous default mix the system is overwhelmingly (and correctly)
abstention-anchored: it spends $84\%$ of its thinking draws on unknown versus $9\%$
target and $7\%$ other, and $11$ of $14$ items sit exactly at the safe default
$[0,0,1]$ (JS-divergence $0$). The mean JS-divergence from that default is only
$0.092$, but it is not zero---three items break ranks (one collapses fully onto
\emph{other} at the maximum $\mathrm{JS}=\ln 2 = 0.693$, two pull toward target at
$0.216$ and $0.380$)---so the abstention default is strong on average yet fragile
per item.

\subsection{Geometry: the answer encodes the task, not the social group}
\label{sec:geometry}

Reading answer-position activations ($n{=}462$ baseline-only items, layer-averaged),
the representational geometry is organized almost entirely by structural task
features rather than social content (\Cref{fig:geometry-sil},
\Cref{fig:geometry-axes}). A silhouette-separability analysis over the full PCA
space shows the answer representation separates most cleanly by
\emph{context\_condition} (ambiguous vs.\ disambiguated, $+0.11$), only weakly by
model accuracy ($+0.02$), gold\_label ($+0.01$), and question\_polarity
($\approx 0.00$), and not at all by bias\_category ($-0.07$). Crucially it
\emph{collapses} on social identity: target\_identity ($-0.42$) and
other\_identity ($-0.43$) silhouettes are strongly negative, meaning points
sharing the same demonstrated social group are no closer than random---the model
encodes ``is this ambiguous or disambiguated'' far more saliently than ``which
group is being judged.'' Every per-label PCA axis in the grid funnels into the
same single arc (PC1 $31\%$ EV, PC2 $28\%$ EV), with the identity axes
($222/223$ distinct values) showing no clustering. This is consistent with the
behavioral readouts on the same $462$ items ($154$ ambiguous, $308$
disambiguated): under three-option forcing the model abstains correctly $72.1\%$
on ambiguous items ($111/154$) but is right only $18.8\%$ when it must commit on
disambiguated items ($58/308$); the two-option readout reaches $60.7\%$
disambiguated ($187/308$) but $0\%$ ambiguous by construction; and the
greedy-thinking decode is right $72.7\%$ ambiguous ($112/154$) vs.\ $41.9\%$
disambiguated ($129/308$), for an overall $52.2\%$ ($241$ correct / $221$
incorrect).

\section{Discussion}

Three findings cut across the studies. First, \emph{abstention and role-calling
are different skills, and a single readout hides the trade-off.} The three-option
teacher-forced readout that best protects abstention on ambiguous items
($71$--$87\%$) is the same one that most cripples role identification on
disambiguated items (down to $28\%$); the two-option forced choice does the
reverse. A model audited on only one of these conditions, or only one readout,
looks far better (or far worse) than it is---which is exactly why we score every
item under three readouts against a per-condition gold. Second, \emph{the bias
lives in the asymmetry, not the average accuracy.} Disambiguated accuracy is low
across the board, but the signal is the target-vs-other gap within it: racismo
and genero leak mass toward the non-target group, clasismo and xenofobia stay
near-symmetric. Third, \emph{scale helps, but the smallest models fail in the
most dangerous way.} Across five models abstention and disambiguated accuracy both
rise monotonically with size, yet \texttt{Llama-3.2-1B} abstains the least
($0.299$, below the $1/3$ chance line) and instead commits confidently to the stereotyped target role---it
substitutes a confident wrong guess for an honest ``unknown,'' the worst failure
mode for a deployed small model. The geometry study explains why behavioral
debiasing is hard at this scale: the answer representation encodes whether an item
is ambiguous or disambiguated ($+0.11$) but is blind to which social group is in
play ($-0.42/-0.43$), so there is no clean ``social group'' direction for a
content-level intervention to act on. The scaffold and dual-readout machinery
remain useful as a measurement tool---the selection study shows thinking edges
non-thinking on abstention ($86\%$ vs.\ $80\%$)---but the picture they reveal is
of a model whose safe-default abstention is strong on average yet fragile per item
and readout.

\section{Limitations and Dual-Use Considerations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Several limitations bound the claims. (i) The headline baseline, stability,
selection, divergence, and geometry studies use a \emph{single}, small model
(\texttt{Qwen3-0.6B}); only the cross-model sweep spans five models, and even
there each family contributes just two sizes, so the ``scale helps'' trend is
suggestive rather than a tight effect estimate. (ii) The scope is Spanish (plus
matched English) and \emph{four} bias categories (racism, xenophobia, classism,
gender), so the findings do not speak to other languages or social axes; the
\textbf{genero} category in particular is severely underpowered ($n{=}18$ total;
$n{=}6$ per disambiguated slice) and its swings should not be over-read. (iii) The
stability, selection, divergence, and geometry studies run on \emph{small}
item pools (e.g.\ stability $n{=}8/16$ items per condition; geometry baseline-only),
so per-item CIs are wide. (iv) The greedy-thinking and sampled-thinking readouts
depend on a parser that can drop or misread free-form draws (e.g.\ $4$ of $47$
divergence prompts and $13$ of $35$ selection items yielded no parsable thinking
draws), biasing $M$ and $p^{\mathrm{th}}$. (v) The geometry analysis is
\emph{correlational}: it shows the answer representation organizes by context
condition and not by social identity, but it does not establish that this
structure \emph{causes} the behavioral asymmetry. (vi) The position$\to$role
remap and bilingual rendering assume the model treats display positions and the
two languages symmetrically; where it does not, those become confounds.

\paragraph{Dual-use and misuse risks.}
The same machinery that measures and improves abstention can be turned against it,
and we flag this explicitly. (a) The debiasing scaffold is trivially
\emph{invertible}: the one-sentence preamble that pushes the model toward
``unknown'' can be rewritten, with no new technique, into a
\emph{bias-amplifying} preamble that pushes the model to commit to the stereotyped
target group---and our results show the target-vs-other asymmetry such a preamble
could exploit. (b) The cross-model finding that small models substitute confident
stereotyped guessing for abstention is itself a misuse signpost: a deployer
choosing the cheapest model inherits the most biased abstention behavior. (c)
Abstention itself can be \emph{gamed}: a model (or deployer) optimizing only the
abstention metric can answer ``unknown'' indiscriminately, which our per-condition
design specifically guards against by also scoring disambiguated items, where
indiscriminate abstention scores $0$. We therefore release the harness as a
measurement tool with these caveats, and report cross-condition and
cross-readout trade-offs as first-class outcomes.

\paragraph{Future improvements.}
Several extensions follow directly. Broaden the cross-model sweep to more sizes
per family and more families, and extend beyond Spanish and the four categories
(powering up \textbf{genero} in particular). Compose and ablate the four scaffold
mechanisms to isolate the active ingredient, and run the full scaffold grid (not
just the baseline) through the selection and geometry pipelines. Move the geometry
analysis from correlational to causal by \emph{steering} activations along the
context-condition axis and watching the abstain-or-commit behavior respond---an
experiment that, by its symmetry, also quantifies the inversion risk above. We see
this design as the start of that line of work, not the end of it.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\section{Conclusion}

We presented a five-study framework that audits, under a per-condition gold and
three readouts, whether a small open-weight LLM both abstains when a Spanish
social-bias question is ambiguous and commits to the correct role when it is
disambiguated. On the full SESGO grid ($n{=}2310$) \texttt{Qwen3-0.6B} abstains
well ($71$--$87\%$) but commits poorly and asymmetrically ($28$--$46\%$), with
the target-vs-other gap carrying the bias signal. A five-model size sweep shows
both behaviors rise monotonically with scale, while the smallest model substitutes
confident stereotyped guessing for abstention---the most dangerous failure mode in
exactly the low-resource settings where small models are deployed. Probing
answer-position activations ($n{=}462$) shows the model encodes the item's task
structure (ambiguous vs.\ disambiguated) but not its social content, which helps
explain why content-level debiasing is hard at this scale. We release the
reproducible design, notation, and harness so these results can be extended.
