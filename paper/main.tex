\begin{abstract}
On ambiguous Spanish \textsc{SESGO} questions naming no specific person the
correct answer is \emph{unknown}; on disambiguated ones a specific role is
correct. We test debiasing \emph{scaffolds}---one-sentence preambles prepended to
the question to induce abstention---using a five-study framework that scores every
item under three readouts (a teacher-forced three-option distribution over
\{target, other, unknown\}, a two-option forced choice, and a greedy reasoning
decode) against a per-condition gold. On \texttt{Qwen3-0.6B}, a full-data run
($n{=}5388$ ambiguous items) selects $s^\star{=}$\texttt{interpretive\_direction},
which drives near-total abstention ($99.9\%$) and beats the no-scaffold baseline
($85.6\%$); one scaffold underperforms baseline, so not all scaffolds help. This
aligns with the geometry (the scaffold is the dominant separable axis, $+0.48$
silhouette) and the causal steering (\Cref{app:steer}): the scaffold works
behaviorally, representationally, and causally. A five-model Qwen/Llama/Mistral
sweep ($n{=}770$/cell) shows abstention and disambiguated accuracy rise with scale.
We release the harness.
\end{abstract}
