\section{Divergence}
\label{app:divergence}

\paragraph{Methods.}
The divergence study zooms in on a single \emph{ambiguous} SESGO item---a Spanish
racism prompt (negative polarity) whose only correct answer is to abstain
($\textsc{gold}=u$)---and probes it exhaustively on the smallest and largest Qwen
(\texttt{Qwen3-0.6B} and \texttt{Qwen3-32B}). For each model we draw $N{=}100$
free-form ``thinking'' generations $g_i \sim \LLM(x)$, parse each to a committed
role $r_i \in \{t, o, u\}$ (target / other / unknown), and over the $M \le N$
parseable draws form the reasoning distribution
\begin{equation}
  p^{\mathrm{th}}_y \;=\; \frac{1}{M}\sum_{i=1}^{M} \mathbf{1}[\, r_i = y \,],
  \qquad y \in \{t, o, u\},
  \label{eq:pth}
\end{equation}
whose unknown mass $p^{\mathrm{th}}_u$ is the empirical abstain rate. Alongside
the sampled-thinking readout we score three teacher-forced readouts on the same
item---the non-thinking three-option choice ($\hat{y}^{3}$, with abstention
available), the non-thinking two-option forced choice ($\hat{y}^{2}$, target vs.\
other, \emph{no} unknown), and the greedy single ``thinking'' rollout
($\hat{y}^{\mathrm{g}}$). Finally, for \emph{each} of the $100$ thinking draws we
record its mean next-token (vocabulary) entropy as a measure of how uncertain the
reasoning itself was, using Shannon entropy over the model's next-token
distribution $q$ at each generated step,
\begin{equation}
  H(q) = -\sum_{v} q_v \log q_v \quad (\text{nats}),
  \label{eq:vocab-entropy}
\end{equation}
averaged over the draw's generated tokens. Comparing the $0.6$B and $32$B
distributions of this per-draw entropy is the headline of the re-scope: it asks
whether the larger model reasons through the ambiguous item with measurably less
token-level uncertainty.

\paragraph{Results.}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
  \item Per-draw vocab entropy (nats, over all $100$ draws): \texttt{Qwen3-0.6B}
    $0.577 \pm 0.049$ (range $0.452$--$0.690$) vs.\ \texttt{Qwen3-32B}
    $0.443 \pm 0.041$ (range $0.354$--$0.535$)---the $32$B model reasons with
    $\approx\!23\%$ lower mean token-level uncertainty and a tighter, fully
    disjoint spread, so scale buys more confident reasoning on this item.
  \item $100$-draw thinking outcome mix $[t, o, u]$: \texttt{Qwen3-0.6B}
    $[6\%, 0\%, 94\%]$ over $62$ parseable draws (abstain rate $94\%$);
    \texttt{Qwen3-32B} $[1\%, 3\%, 96\%]$ over $99$ parseable draws (abstain rate
    $96\%$). Both reason to the safe \emph{unknown} on the large majority of
    tries; the $0.6$B model's non-abstaining mass leans toward the
    \emph{target} group, the $32$B model's is split.
  \item The $0.6$B parser dropped $38$ of $100$ draws as unparseable vs.\ only
    $1$ of $100$ for the $32$B model---the small model's free-form reasoning is
    far harder to read off cleanly.
  \item Four-readout prediction on the item (gold $=u$): both models abstain
    correctly under the readouts where abstention is available---greedy-thinking
    $\hat{y}^{\mathrm{g}}{=}u$ for both; non-thinking three-option
    $\hat{y}^{3}{=}u$ for both (\texttt{Qwen3-0.6B} $p_u{=}0.94$,
    \texttt{Qwen3-32B} $p_u{\approx}1.0$); sampled-thinking
    $\hat{y}^{\mathrm{th}}{=}u$ for both. The only divergence is the
    \emph{forced} two-option readout (no unknown), where the small model is pushed
    onto \emph{target} ($p{=}0.82$) and the large model onto \emph{other}
    ($p{=}0.99$)---a directional split that exists only because abstention was
    removed.
  \item Smallest vs.\ largest: removing the abstain option flips the forced choice
    in opposite directions across scale, but wherever \emph{unknown} is reachable
    both models abstain; the substantive scale effect is in reasoning
    \emph{confidence} (sharply lower, tighter vocab entropy at $32$B), not in the
    answer they land on.
\end{itemize}
