\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\paragraph{Smaller models are more biased.}
\textit{Evidence.} We score every model in the sweep by the \textsc{SESGO} bias score---the
signed distance $\sigma\sqrt{(1-\mathrm{acc})^2 + (F(\text{Target})-F(\text{Other}))^2}$ from
the ideal model (accuracy $1$, $F(\text{Target}){=}F(\text{Other})$), where $F(\text{Target})$
and $F(\text{Other})$ are the shares of \emph{incorrect} answers biased toward each group and
$\sigma$ is positive when the residual error favours the stereotyped Target group
\citep{robles2025sesgo}. Abstention on ambiguous items rises steeply with scale within every
family (\texttt{Qwen3.5} from $0.0$ at $0.8$B to $1.0$ at $27$B), and the error split toward the
marginalized group narrows. In the bias-vs-accuracy view (\Cref{fig:results-bias}), small models
are both less accurate and more biased, while larger models cluster near zero bias at high
accuracy. All rates carry Wilson $95\%$ confidence intervals.\\
\textit{Limitation.} The sweep mixes families and sizes, so we do not cleanly resolve
size from family on every axis.

\plotfig{figures/fig3_bias_alignment_accuracy.png}{fig3_bias_alignment_accuracy.png}{0.99\linewidth}{\textbf{Bias alignment vs.\ accuracy} across the sweep ($17$ model/mode slices), the count-based \textsc{SESGO} metric \citep{robles2025sesgo}: $F(\text{Target})$ and $F(\text{Other})$ are the shares of \emph{incorrect} answers biased toward each group, and each model is a segment at its accuracy spanning its neutral-vs-negative wording bias ($x{<}0$ leans toward the Other group, $x{>}0$ toward the stereotyped Target); the label gives the signed bias score. Left: ambiguous items (accuracy $=$ abstention); right: disambiguated. Small models are less accurate and more biased; large models cluster near zero bias at high accuracy.}{fig:results-bias}

\paragraph{Scale lowers uncertainty.}
\textit{Evidence.} We fork the chain of thought at every token and track the outcome
distribution $O_t$ (\Cref{fig:results-forking}). The larger \texttt{Qwen3-14B} commits
its answer \emph{decisively}: the change-point test finds a single significant
commitment token (at $t{=}270$, Bayes factor ${\approx}10^{9}$), after which the answer
snaps from abstention to a named group. The smaller \texttt{Qwen3-0.6B} instead
accretes its answer gradually, with no single deciding token (Bayes factor $0.023$).
The larger model resolves to a sharp, low-uncertainty commitment.\\
\textit{Limitation.} A single-item case study per model.\\
\textit{Notes.} The forking (red) token is the position where re-sampling a different
token most diverts the final outcome.

\paragraph{Smaller models do not answer consistently.}
\textit{Evidence.} We measure consistency along two separable axes, holding the gold
answer fixed: \emph{order} stability (the answer is unchanged when the target and other
options swap positions) and \emph{label} stability (the answer is unchanged when the
\texttt{a)b)c)} labels become \texttt{x)y)z)}). Both rise with scale across every
family, and the largest model in the sweep (\texttt{Qwen3.5-27B}) reaches
${\approx}0.98$ on both (\Cref{fig:results-stability}). The two axes can diverge: for several
mid-size models a label swap is far more disruptive than a position swap (e.g.\
\texttt{Mistral 3 8B}, order $0.85$ vs.\ label $0.27$), so cosmetic relabelling alone can
flip a model's answer.\\
\textit{Limitation.} Coverage is partial on the largest slices (small $n$).\\
\textit{Notes.} This implies the other studies should filter for unstable answers.

\paragraph{Forced forks expose the same scale trend.}
\textit{Evidence.} Presenting each ambiguous item in \emph{both} target/other orderings and
reading the committed answer, we track three quantities per model
(\Cref{fig:forced-fork}): the diversity of the expected answer distribution (the effective
number of distinct choices an item takes across its two forks), the next-token vocabulary
entropy, and the probability mass on the selected label. Within each family the larger model
commits more sharply---higher selected-label probability and lower answer diversity---echoing
the stability and forking results on an independent forced-choice probe.\\
\textit{Limitation.} Coverage is partial (the sweep is densest on the smaller models).
