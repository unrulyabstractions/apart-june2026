\section{Model Size and SESGO Bias}
\label{sec:bias}

\subsection{Methods}
\label{sec:bias-methods}
\textsc{SESGO}~\citep{robles2025sesgo} is a Spanish, Latin-American-grounded social-bias
benchmark in the BBQ ambiguous/disambiguated tradition, spanning racism, xenophobia, classism,
and gender. Each item is rendered under two crossed factors (a context
$c\in\{\text{ambig},\text{disambig}\}$ and a question polarity
$\in\{\text{neg},\text{nonneg}\}$), with answer options realizing three roles: the stereotyped
\emph{Target} group ($t$), an \emph{Other} group ($o$), and \emph{Unknown} ($u$). The gold
answer depends on the context:

\begin{table}[ht]
  \centering \small
  \caption{\textsc{SESGO} item types and their gold answer.}
  \label{tab:sesgo-gold}
  \begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Context} & \textbf{Item text} & \textbf{Gold answer} \\
    \midrule
    Ambiguous     & names no specific person & \emph{Unknown} (abstain) \\
    Disambiguated & specifies a group        & that group \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

We write $F(\text{Target})$ and $F(\text{Other})$ for the shares of \emph{incorrect} answers
that harm the marginalized Target and the Other group, and define the signed \emph{bias
alignment} $F(\text{Target})-F(\text{Other})$ (positive when errors lean toward the stereotyped
group). Accuracy is the correctness of the committed choice,
\begin{equation}
  p^{\mathrm{nt}}_y = \frac{\exp(\ell_y)}{\sum_{y'} \exp(\ell_{y'})},\quad y,y'\in\{t,o,u\};
  \qquad
  \mathrm{acc} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{Q}|}\sum_{q\in\mathcal{Q}} \mathbf{1}[\,\hat{y}_q = y^\star_q\,],
  \label{eq:acc}
\end{equation}
and \textsc{SESGO} summarizes a condition with a single
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{bias\_score}
    \;=\; \sigma \cdot \sqrt{\,(1 - \mathrm{acc})^2 + \big(F(\text{Target}) - F(\text{Other})\big)^2\,},
  \label{eq:bias-score}
\end{equation}
large when a model is both inaccurate and one-sidedly biased ($\sigma$ a fixed sign/scale
factor). On ambiguous items $F(\text{Target}) + F(\text{Other}) = 1 - \mathrm{acc}$, since every
non-abstention is an error. We compute these count-based quantities from the greedy readout
across the sweep.

\subsection{Results}
\label{sec:bias-results}

\paragraph{Smaller models are more biased (\Cref{fig:results-bias}).}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=3pt,itemsep=3pt]
  \item Abstention accuracy on ambiguous items \textbf{rises steeply with scale} within every
    family (\texttt{Qwen3.5}: $0.0$ at $0.8$B to $1.0$ at $27$B; \texttt{Gemma} and the larger
    \texttt{Mistral} slices abstain near-perfectly).
  \item \textbf{Low accuracy comes with a wide wording span:} the smallest models spread their
    errors across both named groups (e.g.\ \texttt{Llama 3.2 1B}, accuracy $0.15$, alignment
    span $0.65$), while large models collapse to a near-zero span at high accuracy.
  \item The residual \emph{net} lean toward the marginalized Target group is small for every
    model (${\le}0.17$); the scale effect is in accuracy and spread, not a one-sided lean.
  \item \textbf{Limitation:} the sweep mixes families and sizes. All rates carry Wilson $95\%$ CIs.
\end{itemize}
